Production of TEMPO-Oxidized Cellulose Nanofibers (CNFs) and Characterization

Project Summary

The TEMPO (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl) oxidation process is a widely used method for modifying cellulose, as it selectively oxidizes the surface of cellulose fibers without significantly affecting their internal structure. This method is particularly valued for its ability to produce nanofibers with high crystallinity, good water dispersibility, and thermal stability, making it a promising approach for reinforcing materials. By converting cellulose into nanocellulose fibers (CNFs), the TEMPO process enhances the material's structural and functional properties, making it ideal for applications requiring strong and lightweight composites.

In this project, I investigated the production of nanocellulose fibers (CNF) from algae biomass using the TEMPO oxidation method. The resulting TEMPO-oxidized algae CNFs exhibited higher crystallinity and thermal stability compared to plant-based CNF. These nanofibers were characterized using XRD, TGA, FTIR, and SEM to assess their crystallinity index, thermal properties, and morphology, confirming their suitability as reinforcement fillers.
